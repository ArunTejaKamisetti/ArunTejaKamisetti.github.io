The safe path was right there — a pre-placement offer in hand. Here's the reasoning behind walking away from it to bet on the role I actually want.

## The offer

A PPO is the dream outcome of most internships: a guaranteed seat, no placement-season stress, a known team. By every conventional metric, taking it was the right call.

But conventional metrics optimize for *safety*, not *fit*. And I'd spent the internship learning something specific about myself.

## What the internship taught me

At Wipro I owned the evaluation that funneled **~220 small language models down to one** for a client use case. The work that lit me up wasn't the engineering — it was the *decisions*: which capability matters for this user, what latency they'll tolerate, where the safety risk actually lives.

> Picking a model is a product decision in disguise. The best benchmark score loses to the model that fits the user, the budget, and the risk tolerance.

That realization reframed everything. I don't want to *build* the thing someone else specced. I want to *decide what gets built.*

## The bet

So I declined the PPO. The goal is sharp: an **AI Product Manager** role at a frontier lab. That means I'm trading a certain outcome for a harder, less certain one — but one that's aimed at the work I'm actually good at and care about.

Strong opinions, loosely held. If the data tells me I'm wrong, I'll change course fast. But right now the data — my own experience — points one direction.

## What I'm doing about it

- Sharpening product judgment through case studies and model-eval work
- Studying GTM, strategy, and pricing at IIM Kozhikode
- Vibe-coding small tools to stay close to how products actually get built
